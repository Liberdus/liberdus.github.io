# Why We Chose the Slow Path

When we started Liberdus, we made a small number of unusual decisions. We didn't take the largest funding round we were offered. We didn't ship a token before we'd shipped a working product. We didn't promise quarterly returns to anyone, and we didn't write a roadmap that extended past the eighteen-month horizon.

Some of these decisions were strategic. Most of them were temperamental. This is an essay about the temperament.

## The cult of the new release

There's a specific kind of pressure that operates on early-stage protocols, and once you've felt it you can't unfeel it. The pressure says: *ship something every week. Make announcements. Generate news. Keep the price moving.*

The pressure is wrong. Or, more precisely: it's right about a small set of protocols and wrong about the rest. The protocols for which it's right are the ones whose value is essentially attentional — protocols that need to stay top-of-mind to keep users from drifting elsewhere. For everything else, including everything we wanted to build, the pressure to constantly ship is a distraction at best and a destructive force at worst.

We made a decision early on that we would only ship things that we expected to still be in production three years later. That single rule has shaped almost everything else.

> Software, like any living thing, has to grow or it dies. The argument is for a different relationship to growth — slower, more careful, more apologetic.

## What this looks like in practice

Concretely, the slow-path approach means a few things:

- **Audits before announcements.** We don't talk about new features publicly until they've been through full third-party review. This costs us some marketing momentum. We are at peace with that.
- **Documentation as a moral obligation.** If a feature ships without good docs, it didn't really ship. We've delayed launches to get the docs right.
- **No throwaway code.** We avoid expedient short-term solutions that we know we'll have to replace. The cost of doing it right the first time is almost always less than the cost of fixing it later.
- **Small teams, long horizons.** Our engineering team is smaller than most observers expect. This is by design.

## On boredom as a feature

A lot of what we do, day to day, is boring. We refactor. We add tests. We write changelogs. We rewrite documentation that was perfectly fine but could be slightly clearer.

This boredom is a feature. The exciting parts of building infrastructure are almost always the parts where things are going wrong, and the goal is to have as few of those as possible. A boring quarter at Liberdus is a successful quarter.

I sometimes worry that we don't communicate this well externally. From the outside, "we spent the quarter on tooling and tests" sounds less impressive than "we shipped seventeen new features." But the seventeen-new-features quarter is usually followed by a quarter spent fixing the problems caused by shipping seventeen things at once. The tooling-and-tests quarter compounds.

## The long view

The reason we're doing this — the reason for any of it — is that we want Liberdus to be the kind of protocol that's still here in ten years. We want users to be able to depend on us the way they depend on a well-made tool: not because we're constantly proving ourselves, but because we are quietly, reliably *there*.

That's the goal. Slow path, long view, and the kind of work that is most rewarding precisely when no one is watching.

Thank you for being here.
